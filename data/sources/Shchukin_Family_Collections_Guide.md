# The Shchukin Family Collections — A Comprehensive Guide
*Four brothers, four collections, and where to find every image · compiled 13 June 2026*

---

## The family

The Shchukin brothers were sons of **Ivan Vasilyevich Shchukin (1818–1890)**, a self-made Moscow textile magnate of Old-Believer stock whose firm *I. V. Shchukin & Sons* grew into one of Russia's largest textile businesses (a fortune of some 4 million gold roubles), and **Ekaterina Petrovna Botkina**, daughter of the tea-merchant and art patron Pyotr Botkin. Of their ten children, **four** became collectors of the first rank — and between them they touched almost every department of art history, from Scythian gold to Cubism. Three of the four collections are now anchors of major Russian state museums.

This guide treats each collection in turn: what it was, where it is now, its key works, and **exactly where to obtain images** — preferring hi-res.

| Brother | Dates | Collected | Now in |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pyotr** Ivanovich | 1853–1912 | Russian antiquities, textiles, Eastern & applied art | State Historical Museum, Moscow |
| **Sergei** Ivanovich | 1854–1936 | French Impressionism → Cubism | Hermitage & Pushkin (split 1948) |
| **Dmitri** Ivanovich | 1855–1932 | Old Masters (17th-c. Dutch above all) | Pushkin Museum, Moscow |
| **Ivan** Ivanovich | 1869–1908 | Impressionists + Spanish Old Masters | dispersed; some → Pushkin |

> The definitive modern treatment of all four together is the Pushkin Museum's 2019 exhibition **"Shchukin. Biography of a Collection"** (pushkinmuseum.art), which reconstructed each brother's holdings in dedicated halls — the single best scholarly anchor for the family as a whole.

## 1 · Pyotr Ivanovich Shchukin (1853–1912)
### The antiquarian

The eldest collector-brother and, in his own day, the most famous. Pyotr built **"the Shchukin Museum of Russian Antiquities"** in purpose-built premises on Malaya Gruzinskaya Street in Moscow — a vast, deliberately eclectic accumulation of historic relics, icons, brocades and textiles, jewellery, arms and daggers, manuscripts, Persian and Eastern decorative art, and Russian household objects (his contemporaries found its sheer diversity bewildering). In **1905 he donated the entire museum to the Imperial (now State) Historical Museum**, where it remains a foundational holding.

He was also, briefly, a modernist pioneer alongside Sergei: on the **1898 Durand-Ruel visit** the two brothers bought Pissarros side by side — Sergei taking *Avenue de l'Opéra*, **Pyotr taking *Place du Théâtre Français* (1898)**. Pyotr also owned **Renoir's *Nude Seated on a Sofa* (1876)**. These Impressionist works later passed (via the state museums) into the Pushkin/Hermitage orbit.

**Where it is now:** State Historical Museum (Государственный исторический музей), Moscow — Red Square.

**Image sourcing:**
- State Historical Museum online catalogue: **catalog.shm.ru** (per-object, Russian).
- The Russian State Catalogue of the Museum Fund: **goskatalog.ru** (search "Щукин Петр").
- Wikimedia Commons: **Category:State Historical Museum** and **Category:Shchukin Museum** for the building and select objects.
- His Pissarro (*Place du Théâtre Français*) and Renoir (*Nude Seated on a Sofa*) appear on Commons and in the Pushkin holdings — see the Sergei image guide's lookup method.

## 2 · Sergei Ivanovich Shchukin (1854–1936)
### The modernist — covered in full elsewhere

Sergei's collection is documented exhaustively in the companion files: **`Shchukin_Full_Inventory.md`** and **`shchukin_full_inventory.csv`** (259 works — 50 Picasso, 41 Matisse, 17 Gauguin, 16 Derain, 13 Monet, 8 Cézanne, 4 Van Gogh, and the long tail). That CSV now carries per-painting **image links** — direct hi-res where available, plus a Commons lookup for every work. The image-sourcing method is detailed in the section **"Hi-res image sourcing"** below, which applies to all four collections.

In brief: he began with the Impressionists (1898, Monet), moved through Cézanne–Gauguin–Van Gogh (1903–08), then Matisse (from 1906) and Picasso (from 1909); published a 225-number catalogue in 1913; was nationalised on **5 November 1918**; and saw his collection pass through the First Museum of New Western Painting → GMNZI → the **1948 split between the Hermitage and Pushkin**.

## 3 · Dmitri Ivanovich Shchukin (1855–1932)
### The connoisseur of Old Masters

The scholar of the family — "a thoughtful, enlightened collector and connoisseur of art history," in the Pushkin's own words. Dmitri assembled what was called **"Moscow's best collection of Old Masters,"** with a marked preference for **17th-century Dutch painting**. The result is that **the Dutch and Flemish halls of the Pushkin Museum are today largely built from works that were once his.** His taste ran to cabinet pictures — Dutch genre scenes, still lifes, landscapes, portraits, and religious subjects spanning the 15th–19th centuries.

He gave generously even before nationalisation: in **1903 he donated 19 paintings** to the museum then forming at Moscow University (the future Pushkin). The remainder was nationalised after 1918 and absorbed into the Pushkin.

**Representative names in the Dutch/Flemish manner he favoured** (the kind of masters his halls supplied to the Pushkin): Jan van Goyen, Salomon and Jacob van Ruisdael, Willem Kalf, Pieter de Hooch–circle interiors, Gerard ter Borch, Gabriël Metsu, Adriaen van Ostade, Willem Claesz. Heda and Pieter Claesz. (still life), Hendrick Avercamp (winter scenes), and Flemish cabinet works. *(Attribution of individual canvases to Dmitri specifically requires the Pushkin provenance records / the 2019 catalogue — see note below.)*

**Where it is now:** The Pushkin State Museum of Fine Arts (ГМИИ), Moscow — the Dutch & Flemish galleries.

**Image sourcing:**
- Pushkin official site: **pushkinmuseum.art** (per-object, EN/RU; the Dutch galleries are well digitised).
- Wikimedia Commons: **Category:Dutch paintings in the Pushkin Museum** and **Category:Flemish paintings in the Pushkin Museum** (by-artist subcats, many hi-res).
- **Google Arts & Culture** — Pushkin partner page (gigapixel zoom on the major Dutch works).
- CODART's *Dutch and Flemish Art in Russia* (codart.nl) documents the Shchukin Dutch provenance.

## 4 · Ivan Ivanovich Shchukin (1869–1908)
### The Paris aesthete

The youngest and most cosmopolitan — and the catalyst for the others. After studying law at Moscow University he settled in **Paris in 1893**, becoming a writer and critic and the family's eyes on the French avant-garde: **it was Ivan who introduced Pyotr and Sergei to the new French painting.** At his apartment at **91 avenue de Wagram** he kept a celebrated salon frequented by **Rodin, Degas, Renoir, Odilon Redon, Huysmans, the dealer Paul Durand-Ruel**, and the Russian literary–artistic emigration (Chekhov, Merezhkovsky, Voloshin, Igor Grabar, Balmont, Alexandre Benois).

He collected on two fronts: **Impressionist paintings** and **Spanish Old Masters** (El Greco, Goya and their circle). His Impressionist holdings included **Monet's *Lilac in the Sun* (1872)** — a highlight that later entered the Pushkin. Facing financial ruin late in life, he tried to sell his Spanish pictures to the Louvre; doubts cast on some attributions helped precipitate his **suicide in 1908**, and his collection was dispersed at sale.

**Where it is now:** dispersed through the 1908 Paris sales; individual works are scattered across European and American collections, with some Impressionist pieces (e.g. the Monet) reaching the Pushkin via the family/state route.

**Image sourcing:**
- For dispersed works: **artsandculture.google.com** and museum sites of current holders; auction archives (the 1908 Paris sale catalogues, on **gallica.bnf.fr**).
- His Monet *Lilac in the Sun* → Pushkin: see the Commons lookup method below.
- Spanish-school attributions: trace via the **Frick Photoarchive** and the **RKD** for provenance.

## Hi-res image sourcing — method for all four collections

The single most useful technique, applicable to any work in any of these collections:

**1 · Wikimedia Commons direct original-file link.** Commons holds the genuine gigapixel scans. For *any* Commons file, this URL returns the **original full-resolution image**:
`https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/{exact_filename}`
(spaces become underscores). Verified hi-res examples in the Sergei collection: Pissarro *Avenue de l'Opéra* (9499×7626, 24 MB), Puvis *Poor Fisherman* (10515×7637, 30 MB), Renoir *Lady in Black* (8448×10000, 29 MB), Degas *Dancer at the Photographer's* (8442×10942, 31.6 MB).

**2 · Commons MediaSearch finder** (built into the CSV for every Sergei work):
`https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?search={Artist Title}&title=Special:MediaSearch&type=image`

**3 · Bulk Commons categories:**
- `Category:Sergey Schukin's collection` — 46 files + 17 by-artist subcategories.
- `Category:Paintings in the Pushkin Museum` — 78 by-artist subcategories.
- `Category:Google Art Project works in The Pushkin State Museum of Fine Arts` — 42 gigapixel files.

**4 · NeWestMuseum full-image viewer** (the GMNZI reconstruction — best for the Sergei works with a known inventory id):
`https://www.newestmuseum.ru/full/index.php?id={GE_xxxx}&coll=1`

**5 · Museum portals (per-object, authoritative):**
- Hermitage: **hermitagemuseum.org/digital-collection** and **collections.hermitage.ru**
- Pushkin: **pushkinmuseum.art**
- State Historical Museum (Pyotr): **catalog.shm.ru**
- Russian State Catalogue (all): **goskatalog.ru**

**6 · Google Arts & Culture** — Pushkin and Hermitage partner pages offer gigapixel deep-zoom on the masterpieces:
`artsandculture.google.com/partner/the-pushkin-state-museum-of-fine-arts`

## Confidence & gaps (forensic note)

- **Sergei** — fully reconstructed (259 works, sourced to NeWestMuseum/GMNZI; flagships gold-verified with dual ГЭ/ГМНЗИ numbering). High confidence.
- **Pyotr, Dmitri, Ivan** — collection *identity, scope, current location, and key works* are documented and High confidence; **complete object-by-object work-lists are not** publicly consolidated. The authoritative path to full inventories is the **2019 Pushkin "Shchukin. Biography of a Collection" catalogue** (which reconstructed all four) plus the State Historical Museum catalogue for Pyotr. Those are print/portal sources, not open datasets — so the brothers' guides here are framing + key works + sourcing routes, not raisonné-level lists.
- No image URL or inventory number in any file is invented; blanks are deliberate where a value could not be verified.
